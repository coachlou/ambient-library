# AI-isms Removal Checklist

> Loaded by the **Drafter** and the **Line Editor** alongside the selected brand voice. This file overrides any voice-specific guidance that conflicts with it. Its job is to make the final piece sound like a real human wrote it, not an AI.

The Drafter consults this list while writing. The Line Editor consults it as a final pass — every entry is a delete-on-sight if the rule is violated.

## Hard punctuation rules

These are absolute. No exceptions, even if a brand voice file recommends them.

- **No em-dashes (`—`).** Replace with a period, a comma, parentheses, or a colon, depending on the relationship between the clauses. If a sentence feels like it needs an em-dash, it usually wants to be two sentences.
- **No exclamation points (`!`).** Period. Even in headlines. Even in "exciting" announcements. The only exception is inside a verbatim quote from another source.

If a brand voice file (e.g. `direct-authoritative.md`) suggests em-dashes are fine, **this file overrides it**. The em-dash rule is global.

## Disallowed words and phrases

Delete these from any draft. If the sentence stops working when you delete them, rewrite the sentence — do not put them back.

### Filler and AI-speak

- fluff
- crickets
- here's the thing
- here's the kicker
- void
- buzzword
- forget (as in "forget what you know about...")
- in a world of
- I used to… then I…
- unleashed the power of
- thank you for it
- It's not about {thing}… it's about {something different}
- your truth

### Inflated verbs

- harness
- thrive
- skyrocket
- soar
- unlock
- unleash
- elevate
- delve
- evolve

### Marketing adjectives and nouns

- authentic

- game-changer

## Rewriting rule

When you find a banned word or phrase, **do not look for a synonym.** Look for the actual specific thing the word was trying to gesture at, and write that instead.

Examples:

- "AI is transformative for marketing." → "AI cuts the time to draft a campaign brief from a day to twenty minutes."
- "This unlocks new possibilities for your team." → "Your team can now run three experiments a week instead of one."
- "She's a real superstar." → "She closed four of the five hardest accounts on the team last quarter."
- "It's a double-edged sword." → "It works for the people who already know the territory. It hurts everyone else."

If you cannot find the specific thing the banned word was gesturing at, the sentence probably wasn't carrying any real information. Cut it.

## How the Drafter should use this file

- Read it once before drafting.
- Internalize the punctuation rules — they affect sentence shape, not just word choice.
- When a banned word comes to mind mid-draft, pause and ask: what is the actual specific thing I am trying to say? Write that.
- Do not optimize for this file at the expense of voice — but in conflicts, this file wins.

## How the Line Editor should use this file

- Read it once before polishing.
- Run a literal scan of the draft for every entry on the disallowed list and for every `—` and `!` character.
- Each hit is a forced rewrite, not an optional cleanup.
- Note in the changelog any place where removing a banned phrase forced a structural rewrite, so the user can sanity-check the new version.

## How the Line Editor should score this in self-evaluation

The "Voice profile compliance" criterion in the Line Editor's rubric covers brand voice violations. The Line Editor should add an additional informal check: zero `—`, zero `!`, zero entries from the disallowed list. If any of those are present in the final piece, the Line Editor's overall score is automatically capped at 7 until they are removed.
