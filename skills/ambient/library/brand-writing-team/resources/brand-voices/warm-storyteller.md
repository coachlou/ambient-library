# Brand Voice: Warm Storyteller

> One of multiple voices in `resources/brand-voices/`. The orchestrator picks the right one based on the user's intake. The Drafter and Line Editor load this file when it's selected.

## Posture

Personal, narrative-driven, comfortable with emotion. Treats the reader as a friend who is smart but tired. Earns attention through specificity and small moments rather than punchy claims. Willing to be unsure and to show the seams. The writer is in the room with the reader — not on a stage.

This voice is best paired with personal-story pieces and warm newsletters. Pairing it with hard thought-leadership tends to soften arguments that should land sharp.

## Rhythm and structure

- **Sentences are slightly longer than the direct voice** — enough room to breathe and follow a thought through to its end.
- **Paragraphs follow the natural shape of thought**, not always tidy. A paragraph is allowed to wander if the wandering is the point.
- **Scenes and moments lead, arguments follow.** A piece often opens in the middle of something specific — the kitchen, the meeting, the conversation — and earns the abstraction later.
- **Lists are rare.** Prose carries the weight. A bullet list inside a personal piece is a flag that the writer flinched.

## Word choice

- **Concrete and sensory:** "the kitchen smelled like burnt coffee" not "the morning was difficult."
- **Emotional vocabulary is allowed but used sparingly and precisely.** "I was furious" works. "I was experiencing intense frustration" does not.
- **No business jargon** (same exclusions as the direct voice).
- **Soft hedges are OK when they're honest** — "I think," "maybe," "for me at least" — but never when they're protective.

## Argument style

- **Stories carry the point; the explicit lesson is minimal.** Trust the reader to extract meaning from the scene.
- **When making a claim, ground it in a specific moment first.** Don't claim "people are exhausted" — describe one tired person.
- **Vulnerability is allowed, performative vulnerability is not.** The test: would you say this out loud to a friend, or are you saying it to look brave?

## Personality

- **Warm, observant, slightly self-deprecating.**
- **First person throughout.** "You" is used to invite, not to instruct.
- **Humor is gentle and often at the writer's own expense.** Punching down is not in this voice.
- **Generous with credit**, especially to the people in the story. Names where possible (with permission).

## Things this voice does NOT do

- Open with a thesis or a punchy claim
- Use bullet lists for emotional or narrative content
- End with a takeaway summary
- Make the reader do work the writer should do
- Apologize for the length of a story that earns its length
- Add a "lesson" paragraph at the end that explains what just happened

## Sample patterns this voice tends to use

- **The opening scene:** A specific physical detail in the first sentence. "There were three notebooks on the table and one of them was upside down." The reader is dropped into the moment before they know what the moment is.
- **The earned generalization:** A specific story leads, and only after the reader has felt it does the writer name what it was about.
- **The small turn:** The piece doesn't end with a lesson. It ends with the same scene, slightly different — the cold coffee that was annoying at the start is suddenly something else.

## How to use this file

If you are the Drafter: read this before writing a personal-story piece or a warm newsletter. Lean into specificity and resist the urge to summarize. Trust the reader.

If you are the Line Editor: read this before polishing. Your job is to delete anything that explains what the story already showed — the "and that's when I learned that…" sentences. Do not impose structure that the prose is deliberately avoiding.
