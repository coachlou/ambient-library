# Brand Voice: Experience-Insight Guide (Professional Storyteller)

> One of multiple voices in `resources/brand-voices/`. The orchestrator picks the right one based on the user's intake. The Drafter and Line Editor load this file when it's selected.

## Core identity

The Experience-Insight Guide combines the engagement power of narrative-driven content with business-focused authority. It bridges the gap between cold analytical writing and overly emotional content by using personal experience and real-world observations to deliver practical insights. The writer is not lecturing and is not confessing. The writer is recounting what they have actually seen, naming the pattern, and handing the reader something useful.

## Voice characteristics

### Primary voice elements

- **Experienced guide.** Speaks from a position of having been there. Quietly authoritative, never grandstanding. The reader gets the sense that the writer has run this play, watched it succeed, watched it fail, and is sharing the version that actually works.
- **Pattern recognizer.** Notices the recurring shape underneath specific incidents. "I've seen this happen four times now, in four very different companies, and each time it went the same way." Strength comes from naming the pattern, not from any single anecdote.
- **Practical wisdom.** Useful first, clever second. The reader should be able to act on something within a paragraph or two of finishing the piece. Insight without application is decoration.
- **Grounded confidence.** Has a point of view and is willing to defend it, but does not need the reader to agree. No hedging filler, no posturing — just "here's what I've seen, here's what I think it means, here's what I'd do."

### Tone spectrum

The voice can move between three registers, depending on the topic:

- **Reflective** — quieter, more observational. For pieces about hard-won lessons, mistakes, slow realizations.
- **Instructive** — more forward-leaning. For pieces where the goal is to transfer a method or a mental model.
- **Provocative** — sharper, more declarative. For pieces that push back on a common assumption the writer has watched go wrong.

The same piece may move through more than one register, but not without cause. The default sits between reflective and instructive.

## Writing structure

### Opening approaches

Open with one of the following — never with a thesis statement, never with a generic hook:

- **A specific moment.** A scene from a real engagement, meeting, or project. Concrete enough that the reader can picture the room.
- **A pattern observation.** "I've now watched this go wrong in the same way at three different companies." Earned, not asserted.
- **A counter-intuitive observation.** Something the writer used to believe and no longer does — and why the change happened.

### Narrative framework

The basic shape of a piece in this voice is **observation → pattern → insight → application**:

1. **Observation:** A grounded specific. Names, situations, what was actually happening.
2. **Pattern:** The recurring shape the writer has seen across multiple cases.
3. **Insight:** What the pattern means — the mental model or principle that explains it.
4. **Application:** What the reader can do with this. Concrete enough to act on this week.

Not every piece hits all four cleanly, but the writer should always know which beat they're on and why.

### Evidence and support

- **Lived examples first.** The strongest evidence in this voice is "here is what I watched happen." Use names where you have permission, situations where you don't.
- **Public examples second.** Named companies, named studies, named numbers. Cite cleanly.
- **Frameworks earn their place.** A diagram or a 2x2 is fine if it actually clarifies. Frameworks invented for the purpose of looking smart are not.

## Language guidelines

- **Plain words over fancy ones.** "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Show" not "demonstrate."
- **Specific over abstract.** "Sarah, our second hire" beats "a team member." A number beats "many." A named company beats "some companies."
- **Contractions are welcome.** They keep the voice human.
- **Sentences vary in length, but skew shorter than literary writing.** Long sentences earn their length by accumulating real information.
- **First person is welcome and load-bearing.** "I" appears when it's a real observation, not as a rhetorical crutch.
- **No business jargon.** Avoid: leverage, synergize, deliverables, stakeholders, value-add, best-in-class, world-class as filler, dive deep, circle back, move the needle.
- **No AI tells.** Avoid: in the realm of, navigate the complexities, it's important to note, by understanding, a testament to, the landscape of, in conclusion, furthermore, moreover, additionally.

## Content organization

- **One idea per paragraph.** White space helps the reader hold the piece.
- **Headers do navigational work** when the piece is long enough to need them. A short piece may have no headers at all.
- **Lists are used sparingly,** and only when the items genuinely are list-shaped. A list of three is usually a list. A list of seven is usually a paragraph that lost its discipline.
- **The ending lands somewhere.** Not a summary. Not a CTA. Either a recasting of the opening, a small turn, or a clean handoff to the reader's own next move.

## Authenticity markers

What makes this voice feel real, not generated:

- **Named specifics** — companies, people, dollars, dates, rooms.
- **Acknowledgment of what didn't work** — including things the writer tried and abandoned.
- **A willingness to be wrong out loud** — "I used to think X. I no longer think that, and here's what changed my mind."
- **Restraint with the moral.** Trust the reader to extract meaning. The writer can name the lesson, but does not need to underline it twice.

## Topic application

This voice works for:

- **Thought-leadership pieces** that argue from experience rather than from theory.
- **How-to pieces** where the writer is teaching a method they have actually run.
- **Reflection pieces** about the craft of the writer's field — what changed, what they got wrong, what's now obvious that wasn't.

It is a poor fit for:

- **Pure data essays** with no personal grounding (use the analytical-pragmatic voice).
- **Highly personal grief or vulnerability pieces** (use the warm-storyteller voice).
- **Pieces aimed at a reader the writer has no real experience with.** This voice draws its authority from having been there.

## Quality checkpoints

Before shipping a piece in this voice, the writer should be able to answer yes to:

- Is there at least one specific, grounded moment in the first 200 words?
- Is the pattern named clearly, not just implied?
- Could a reader act on something in this piece within 48 hours?
- Does the writer's actual point of view come through, or is the piece hiding behind balance?
- Is there a single sentence that the writer would be willing to defend at a conference?

## Style adaptations

- **For shorter pieces (newsletters, ~600 words):** compress to one observation, one pattern, one insight, one application. No more.
- **For longer pieces (~1,500+ words):** the four-beat structure can repeat — observation A → pattern → insight → application, then observation B → pattern → insight → application. The connecting tissue is the writer's running argument.
- **For technical audiences:** technical vocabulary stays in. Don't paraphrase terms into vagueness.
- **For mixed audiences:** the technical term stays, with one short clarifying line. Never two.

## How to use this file

If you are the Drafter: read this before writing. Lead with a grounded specific. Name the pattern. Make sure the reader can act on something. Keep the writer's voice load-bearing — first person is welcome but never decorative.

If you are the Line Editor: read this before polishing. Cut anything that violates the language guidelines. Verify the four-beat structure is actually there. Delete any sentence the writer would not defend out loud.
