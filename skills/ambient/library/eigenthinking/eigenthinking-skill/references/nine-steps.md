# Nine Steps: Full Protocol

The complete Eigenthinking step-by-step guide with prompts, practices, 
completion signals, and failure-mode guards for each step.

## Table of Contents

   - Gate: Market Novelty Checkpoint (between DRILL and REFRAME)
3. REFRAME — Build the lens

## Step 1 — SIGNAL
*Find the friction worth following.*

### What it is
Not every irritant is a signal. Some are noise. SIGNAL is the practice of 
distinguishing structural friction (a gap between how things work and how they 
should at a mechanism level) from surface friction (a bad tool, a slow process, 
a difficult client).

Structural friction has a specific fingerprint: it recurs across different contexts, 
it resists the obvious fix, and it bothers you more than it bothers others in your 
field. That last part — your heightened sensitivity — is diagnostic. It points 
toward one of your cognitive eigenvectors.

### The Practice
- Ask the user to describe what keeps bothering them — before they try to fix it
- Look for recurrence ("I keep seeing this..."), resistance ("I've tried X and it 
  didn't work..."), and disproportionate irritation ("why doesn't everyone see this?")
- Hold back from problem-solving; stay in observation mode

### The Friction Log
Recommend users keep a running friction log: one sentence per irritant, written 
before attempting a fix. Monthly review reveals patterns that single entries hide.

### With AI
> "Describe the recurring friction in plain language — what you expected, what 
> you got, why the gap bothers you. Before we try to fix it, let's understand 
> what's actually causing it."

### Completion Signal
The friction statement feels uncomfortably accurate — like naming something that 
was hiding in plain sight. The user often responds with "exactly" or "that's it."

**Output:** One sentence — "The real problem is ___."
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

### Failure Mode Guard
Watch for symptom descriptions masquerading as friction statements. "My clients 
don't implement my advice" is a symptom. "There's a structural gap between 
information transfer and behavioral change in professional consulting" is friction.

## Step 2 — DRILL
*Diagnose the mechanism, not the symptom.*

### What it is
Once you have the friction, resist the first available solution. Drill down to 
the mechanism: what structural condition makes this friction inevitable? What would 
have to be true at a deeper level for this problem to keep recurring regardless 
of surface fixes?

This is where domain expertise becomes irreplaceable. AI can bring cross-domain 
frameworks and adjacent analogies. The user brings the instinct that knows which 
ones actually apply.

### The Drilling Protocol
Keep asking "what mechanism causes this?" until the answer is a property of the 
system, not a property of a specific instance.

Example drilling sequence:
- "My clients don't implement my advice."
- Why? "They understand it but don't do it."
- What mechanism causes that? "Information doesn't automatically translate to behavior."
- Why not? "Behavior change requires identity shift, not just cognitive update."
- *Stop here — this is structural.*

### The Adjacent Domain Move
Explicitly ask AI to pull frameworks from fields outside the user's own domain:
- Legal thinking applied to business strategy
- Ecology applied to market dynamics  
- Physics applied to organizational behavior
- Mathematics applied to cognition

Cross-domain structural analogies are where the most original diagnoses live.

### With AI
> "Before we discuss solutions, help me identify the structural mechanism causing 
> [friction]. What would have to be true at a system level for this to keep 
> happening regardless of what I try? Pull in frameworks from adjacent domains 
> if they're relevant."

Stress-test prompt:
> "What would have to be true for this diagnosis to be wrong? What's the strongest 
> counter-argument?"

### Completion Signal
A knowledgeable peer in the user's field says (or the user imagines they'd say): 
"I never thought of it that way — but that's exactly right."

**Output:** A structural explanation that satisfies a smart skeptic. Not just
"the problem is X" but "the problem is X because of Y, and Y exists because of Z."
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

### Failure Mode Guard
If the diagnosis sounds like common wisdom in the field, drill further. 
Original IP comes from structural insights the field has overlooked, not 
from repackaging what's already known.

## Gate: Market Novelty Checkpoint
*Run between DRILL and REFRAME. Not a step — a gate.*

### What it is
Before building a reframe, verify that the structural diagnosis is not
already claimed territory in the user's specific market. AI cannot reliably
distinguish novel insight from well-marketed conventional wisdom because
both exist in its training data. This gate forces an explicit check.

### The Protocol

**1. Ask the user to name the competitive landscape.**
> "Who are the top 5–10 voices, firms, or methodologies in your specific
> market? What do they claim as their differentiator? What language do they
> use to describe the problem you're solving?"

The user's domain expertise is the primary market intelligence source.
They know what is being claimed better than any search can determine.

**2. Map the claimed territory.**
From the user's input, build a simple landscape:

| Competitor/Voice | Their Claimed Mechanism | Their Language |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------------|
| [Name] | [What they say causes the problem] | [Key phrases] |
| [Name] | [What they say causes the problem] | [Key phrases] |

**3. Test the user's diagnosis against the map.**
For each entry, ask: "Is your structural diagnosis saying something
different from this — or the same thing in different words?"

If the user's diagnosis overlaps with claimed territory, say so directly:
> "Your current diagnosis — [X] — is close to what [Competitor] already
> claims. That doesn't mean it's wrong, but it means the REFRAME needs to
> find where your diagnosis diverges from theirs. What do you see that
> they're missing?"

**4. If available, use web search to supplement.**
Search for the user's market + their key mechanism terms. Look for:
- Published frameworks using similar language
- Conference talks or books making the same structural claim
- LinkedIn/blog posts from competitors articulating similar mechanisms

Report findings to the user: "Here's what I found being claimed in your
space. How does your view differ?"

**5. The honest admission.**
If the diagnosis does not clearly differ from what's already claimed in
the market, say: "I can't verify that this is novel in your market based
on what I can see. That doesn't mean it isn't — you know your market
better than I do. But we should find the specific point of divergence
before we build a reframe on it."

### Completion Signal
The user can articulate — in one sentence — what their diagnosis says
that no competitor in their market is saying. If they can't, return to
DRILL and dig deeper.

### Failure Mode Guard
Do not accept "my version is more nuanced" or "I do it better" as
novelty. Those are execution advantages, not structural novelty.
The test is: does the diagnosis name a mechanism that competitors
are NOT naming?

## Step 3 — REFRAME
*Build the new lens.*

### What it is
A structural diagnosis tells you what's wrong. A reframe tells you how to see 
it differently — in a way that makes the solution visible and changes what 
questions are worth asking.

The reframe is not the solution. It's the cognitive shift that makes the right 
solution findable. 

Good reframes have three properties:
1. **Counterintuitive** — the obvious framing was wrong or incomplete
2. **Structural** — applies across many instances, not just this one
3. **Generative** — once you have the lens, new solutions appear that weren't visible before

### The Practice
State the conventional framing of the problem. Then systematically challenge it:
- What if the opposite were true?
- What if the constraint were actually the resource?
- What if what looks like a problem is actually a feature of the solution?
- What if the unit of analysis is wrong (too small, too large, wrong level)?

### With AI
> "Here's the conventional way this problem is framed: [X]. Help me find framings
> that are structurally incompatible with this one — where if the alternative is
> correct, the conventional approach is not just suboptimal but wrong."

### The Candidate Protocol (mandatory for REFRAME)

Do not converge on a single reframe. Generate a minimum of 4 candidate
reframes that are structurally incompatible with each other. Present them
as a numbered set with a one-sentence description of each:

> "Here are four candidate reframes. Each implies a different methodology
> downstream. Which one — if any — matches what you actually see in the
> field? Or does the right reframe combine elements, or lie outside all
> of these?"

**Use the full non-modal arsenal for candidate generation.** The techniques
in `references/cognitive-patterns.md` are the skill's most powerful tools
for producing genuinely divergent reframes. Employ them explicitly:

- **Structural Incompatibility Prompt** — generate positions that cannot
  coexist; if one is correct, the others are wrong, not just suboptimal
- **Shadow Harvest** — what do credible contrarians in this field actually
  believe? What's the suppressed-but-correct position?
- **Adjacent Domain Import** — how would a geologist, mathematician, or
  ecologist diagnose this same problem? What lens from their field applies?
- **Boundary Probe** — push each candidate to its logical extreme; where
  does it break, and what's on the other side?
- **Faction Analysis** — whose worldview does each reframe represent?
  What intellectual tradition produces each one?

**At least one candidate must reframe from the audience's perspective** —
not "here's what I do differently" but "here's what you believe that's
wrong, and what it's costing you." The audience-perspective reframe often
produces the sharpest candidate because it forces the expert to diagnose
the client's misconception, not just describe their own method.

**Evaluation criteria for each candidate:**
- Does it pass the behavioral test? (Changes what you'd do next)
- Is it already claimed in the user's market? (Market novelty check)
- Is it genuinely counterintuitive, or just contrarian?
- Does it generate new solutions that weren't visible before?

**The selection belongs to the user.** AI generates the landscape; the
expert's domain knowledge selects from it. Never present a single reframe
as "the" answer. The user's selection — and their reason for it — often
reveals the real reframe that none of the candidates captured exactly.

### Completion Signal
The reframe changes what the user would do next — not just how they'd describe 
what they're already doing. If the reframe only changes language, it's not a 
reframe yet.

**Output:** One sentence that captures the new lens. Often takes the form
"Instead of [conventional approach], [reframe]."
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

### Examples from practice
- "Instead of asking AI for the answer, map the answer space first." (Latent Cartographer)
- "Instead of protecting information, capture and transfer the process." (UP → IP)
- "Instead of finding your natural strengths, find your natural cognitive axes." (Eigenthinking)

## Step 4 — NAME
*Make the concept portable.*

### What it is
An unnamed insight is a thought. A named concept is a tool. The name is the 
container that makes the insight transferable, teachable, searchable, and ownable.

Good names do six things simultaneously:
1. Create a curiosity gap (people want to know what it means)
2. Hint at the mechanism (the name teaches while it labels)
3. Are ownable (no one else uses it for this concept)
4. Are competitively durable (see below)
5. Are short enough to repeat naturally (2–3 words ideal)
6. Come from an unexpected domain (creates freshness through surprise)

**Competitive durability (criterion 4):** If a direct competitor adopted this
exact name and language tomorrow, would the market still associate it with you?
A name coupled to a specific structural mechanism ("Eigenthinking" only makes
sense if you understand eigenvectors as cognitive axes) is durable. A name that
describes a general quality ("The Living Answer") is a slogan — anyone can claim
it. The test: does the name *require* the methodology to make sense? If the name
is detachable from the structural mechanism, tighten the coupling.

### Prior Name Check

If the Entry-Point Assessment confirmed an existing name, do not regenerate
candidates. Run the verification template instead:

**NAME Verification Template (use when prior name exists):**

Begin your response with: "This name was confirmed in prior work. My role is to
verify it holds, not to re-evaluate from scratch."

Then verify against seven criteria using PASS/FAIL only — never numeric scores:

1. Curiosity gap: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
2. Mechanism hint: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
3. Ownability: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
4. Competitive durability: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
5. Brevity: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
6. Domain freshness: PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]
7. Speakability in user's working language(s): PASS/FAIL — [one sentence]

If all pass: "Name holds. Advancing." Confirm with user and move to next
incomplete step.

If any fail: Name the failing criterion. Propose a minimal modification
addressing that specific criterion — not a full regeneration.

Critical: use PASS/FAIL, never numeric scores. Numeric scores on prior decisions
create ambiguous results that trigger re-evaluation spirals.

### The Five-Domain Protocol
When naming from scratch, generate name candidates from five distinct metaphorical
domains before evaluating any of them. Domains to draw from:

| Domain | Why it works | Examples |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| Mathematics | Precision + elegance | Eigenthinking, Vector, Gradient |
| Geology/Geography | Depth + terrain + discovery | Fault Line, Cartographer, Seam |
| Navigation | Direction + purposeful movement | Waypoint, Bearing, Heading |
| Biology/Ecology | Living systems + emergence | Signal, Membrane, Mycelium |
| Physics | Forces + transformation | Eigenvector, Resonance, Lever |

### The Selection Test
Say the name out loud to someone unfamiliar with the concept. 

- First response "what does that mean?" → you have a name
- First response "oh, like [something they already know]" → keep looking

If the user operates in multilingual markets or is a non-native English speaker,
test speakability in all working languages. A name that scores perfectly on
analytical criteria but cannot be spoken naturally in the user's primary working
language fails portability. This is a disqualifying gate.

### With AI
> "Help me name this concept: [reframe in one sentence]. Generate five name
> candidates from five completely different metaphorical domains — mathematics,
> geology, navigation, biology, physics. Then evaluate each against: curiosity
> gap, mechanism hint, ownability, competitive durability, brevity, freshness."

When naming from scratch, generate at least 15 candidates before selecting. The
best name often isn't in the first five.

### Completion Signal
Saying the name out loud creates a mandatory explanation moment every time.

**Output:** A name + a one-sentence definition that can follow it.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

## Step 5 — BUILD
*Make it followable.*

### What it is
The methodology turns the reframe into a system someone else can execute. This 
is where tacit expertise becomes explicit IP — and where most knowledge 
entrepreneurs stop too soon.

A complete methodology has five properties:
1. **Sequential** — steps have an order for structural reasons, not arbitrary ones
2. **Named at every level** — each phase has a label that does cognitive work
3. **Testable** — you can tell when each step is done (completion signal)
4. **Bounded** — clear beginning and end
5. **Failure-aware** — anticipates where people go wrong and builds in guardrails

### The Articulation Protocol
Ask the user to describe what they actually do when working at their best — 
out loud, unfiltered, in sequence. Don't edit for elegance yet. Then structure:

1. Identify distinct phases (where does the nature of the work change?)
2. Name each phase (imperative verb + noun: "Surface the Friction", "Go Structural")
3. Sequence them (which must precede which, and why?)
4. Articulate the completion signal for each
5. Identify the transition condition between phases

### With AI
> "I'm going to describe my process for [X]. As I do, help me identify the 
> distinct phases, name them, sequence them, and articulate what 'done' looks 
> like at each stage. Don't edit for elegance — capture what's actually happening."

### The Failure-Mode Pass
After drafting the methodology, run this explicitly:
> "Where would a smart person following this get stuck? What's the most common 
> failure mode at each phase? What's missing?"

The answers are always worth adding.

### Completion Signal
Someone with no access to the user's tacit knowledge could follow the methodology 
and produce something recognizably theirs.

**Output:** A complete, named, sequenced methodology with quality gates at each step.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

## Step 6 — STRESS TEST
*Break it before the market does.*

### What it is
Adversarial review before publishing. Four moves:

**Red Team** — the strongest argument that the methodology is wrong.  
Ask: "What's the best case that this methodology produces bad outcomes? 
Under what conditions does it fail completely?"

**Edge Case** — the situation where the methodology breaks down.  
Ask: "Where does this work well but not always? What context makes it inapplicable?"

**Honesty Check** — the most important move.
Ask: "Where in this methodology could someone go through the motions and produce
something that looks like output without having done the real thinking?"

The Honesty Check finds the step where compliance is performable. That step needs
a quality gate that performed compliance fails.

**Falsifiability Check** — the intellectual rigor move.
Ask: "What evidence — not testimonial, but structural — would make a knowledgeable
skeptic concede this methodology produces different results than the standard
approach? If no such evidence is even designable, the methodology may be
unfalsifiable — it can't be proven wrong, but it also can't be proven right.
That's a different kind of vulnerability."

The Falsifiability Check tests whether the methodology makes claims that could
be validated. An unfalsifiable methodology isn't necessarily wrong — but it's
indistinguishable from one that is.

### With AI
> "Act as a skeptical expert trying to disprove this methodology. Identify the 
> three most vulnerable points — where it relies on assumptions that could fail, 
> and what the failure scenario looks like for each."

### The Delta Report Principle
Every methodology needs an honesty mechanism — something that prevents the process 
from becoming theater. Build in a step that explicitly asks: "Did this process 
change anything? If not, say so."

If the output of a step looks the same whether the step was done or skipped, 
the step has no quality gate. Add one.

### Completion Signal
You found at least one thing to add — a guardrail, quality gate, or missing step — 
that you didn't know was missing before the stress test.

**Output:** A hardened methodology with identified failure modes documented and
guardrails added.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

## Step 7 — COMPRESS
*Prove the thinking with compression.*

### What it is
Compression is a quality test, not a formatting exercise. If the thinking can't 
survive reduction — if something essential is lost when you compress it — the 
thinking isn't fully understood yet. Return to DRILL.

Compression levels (work down sequentially):
1. **Full methodology** — complete document with all phases
2. **One paragraph** — structural explanation a practitioner could navigate from
3. **Three sentences** — mechanism + what it produces + why it matters
4. **One sentence** — creates desire to know more
5. **A phrase** — the name + a verb (optional but powerful when achievable)

### The One-Sentence Test
The one-sentence version must do two things: create desire to know more, AND 
hint at the mechanism. If it's just catchy but doesn't hint at how it works, 
it's a tagline, not a compression.

Test: Could a practitioner reconstruct the methodology from the one-sentence 
version? Not completely — but could they orient themselves enough to know 
what kind of thinking is involved?

### With AI
> "Help me compress this methodology through five levels: full document → 
> one paragraph → three sentences → one sentence → phrase. At each level, 
> test: does this still point someone toward the right practice?"

### Completion Signal
The one-sentence version creates desire to know more, and the one-paragraph 
version could serve as a navigation aid through the full methodology.

**Output:** One-sentence version + one-paragraph version + confirmed that
compression doesn't lose the mechanism.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

## Step 8 — LIFT
*Move one abstraction level up.*

### What it is
The lift question: what does this methodology generalize to? What is it an 
instance of? What larger pattern does it exemplify?

Every lift creates a new level of IP:
- The methodology is valuable
- The principle it exemplifies is more valuable  
- The category it defines is most valuable of all

### Examples of the lift
| Methodology | Lift (principle instantiated) |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| Latent Cartographer | Map before move |
| UP → IP | Process is more valuable than information |
| Eigenthinking | Your cognitive patterns are your most leverageable asset |

### The Meta-Move
After naming the lifted principle, check: does the lift reveal a gap in the 
original methodology? Something it assumed but didn't address, visible from 
the higher level? If yes, return to BUILD and add it.

### With AI
> "What is this methodology an example of? If I named the class of methods 
> it belongs to, what would that class be called? And does seeing it from 
> that level reveal anything the methodology is missing?"

### Completion Signal
The lifted principle is more broadly applicable than the methodology it came from. 
Other practitioners in different fields could apply the principle even if they 
can't use the methodology directly.

**Output:** The named principle the methodology instantiates, stated in one sentence.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.

## Step 9 — TEACH
*Produce the artifact that transfers it.*

### What it is
The methodology exists in the user's head until an artifact makes it transferable. 
The artifact is not documentation — it's a demonstration. The best teaching 
artifacts don't just describe the methodology; they enact it.

### The Multi-Role Production Pipeline
Assign roles explicitly. Don't ask AI to "write about this." 

| Role | Question it answers |
|------|-------------------|
| Researcher | What angles matter most for this audience? What's the freshest entry point? |
| Strategist | What's the central tension? How does it resolve? What's the one-sentence thesis? |
| Writer | What voice serves this audience? What's the opening that creates the curiosity gap? |
| Editor | Where does it drag? Where does it overreach? Where is the mechanism buried? |
| Publisher | What platform? What packaging? What's the call to action? |

See `references/production-pipeline.md` for the full AIMM integration protocol.

### Voice as Strategic Decision
The same methodology communicated in authoritative third-person reaches a 
different audience than a personal first-person account of discovery. 
Neither is generically better. Ask:

- Is the user's credibility source their expertise (third-person authority) 
  or their journey (first-person vulnerability)?
- Does the audience want to learn from an expert or identify with a peer?
- What's the platform's trust context?

### The Organic Insight Rule
During production, new connections surface — moments where the artifact reveals 
something the methodology didn't fully capture. These are not distractions. 
They're the UP asserting itself through the production process.

When this happens:
1. Pause production
2. Ask: "Where does this naturally belong?"
3. Find its structural home in the existing artifact
4. Fold it in — don't force it, don't dismiss it

### Completion Signal
The artifact does something the user couldn't have produced before the deep work. 
It demonstrates the methodology rather than describing it. The reader experiences 
the thinking.

**Output:** A finished artifact — article, SOP, framework document, skill,
course module, or workshop — that a target audience can engage with immediately.
Write the confirmed output to the working document.
