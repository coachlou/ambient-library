## 1. First Principles

**Function:** DECONSTRUCT
**Core:** Strip away assumptions until you reach foundational truths that cannot be further decomposed.
**Unique power:** Reveals when the entire framing is wrong — when shared assumptions are unquestioned assumptions.
**Blind spot:** Deconstructs endlessly without rebuilding. Misses emergent properties.
**Signature question:** "What would I believe about this if I had zero prior knowledge and could only work from what's directly observable?"

### Moves

**Five Whys** (Toyota/Ohno) — Ask "why?" iteratively 5 times to move from surface symptoms to root causes. Best for operational problems with causal chains. Limitation: Assumes linear causation; struggles with multiple interacting causes.

**Socratic Questioning** (Plato) — Probe with targeted questions to expose contradictions and unstated assumptions. Six types: clarification, probing assumptions, probing evidence, questioning viewpoints, probing implications, questioning the question. Best for examining beliefs and argument coherence.

**Cartesian Doubt** (Descartes) — Systematically doubt everything that can possibly be doubted. Whatever survives radical doubt is genuinely foundational. Best for examining the foundations of an entire belief system. Limitation: Can lead to paralysis.

**Zeroth Principle** — Before questioning assumptions about the problem, question assumptions about WHAT THE PROBLEM IS. Examine meta-assumptions. Best for when conventional first-principles keeps producing unsatisfying results, suggesting the frame itself is wrong.

**Physics-Style Reasoning** (Musk) — Identify physical constraints (not economic, not conventional). Work backward from what the laws of physics permit. Best for engineering and cost-reduction where convention has drifted from physical limits. Limitation: Only applicable where physical constraints are binding.

**Assumption Mapping** — Explicitly list every assumption in a plan. Categorize as: confirmed, unconfirmed, or critical (if wrong, everything collapses). Attack critical unconfirmed ones first. Best for business plans, strategies, investment theses.

**Regressive Abstraction** — Ask "what is this an instance of?" repeatedly until you reach the most general principle. Then check whether the abstraction reveals something invisible at the specific level. Best for finding hidden connections between seemingly different problems.

**Via Negativa** (Taleb) — Instead of "what should I add?", ask "what should I remove?" Knowledge grows more reliably by eliminating what's false than by asserting what's true. Best for decision-making, product design, lifestyle design.

## 2. Falsification

**Function:** EVALUATE
**Core:** Actively attempt to prove a belief wrong by deriving observable consequences and checking whether they hold.
**Unique power:** Delivers decisive refutation. Can kill an idea cleanly and permanently.
**Blind spot:** Can only destroy — cannot generate. Requires clear, testable predictions.
**Signature question:** "What evidence would prove this wrong? Does that evidence exist?"

### Moves

**Pre-Mortem** (Gary Klein) — Before executing, imagine it has already failed. Work backward: "What went wrong?" Best for project planning, strategy launch, investment decisions. Limitation: Prospective — works with imagined failure, not actual evidence.

**Reductio ad Absurdum** — Assume the proposition is true, follow implications rigorously. If they lead to contradiction, the proposition is false. Best for testing logical consistency of arguments and policies.

**Crucial Experiment** (Bacon/Popper) — Design a single experiment whose outcome decisively distinguishes between two competing hypotheses. Best for A/B testing decisions and scientific disputes.

**Devil's Advocate** — Deliberately argue against a position, especially one you agree with, to expose weaknesses. Distinct from Steelmanning: Devil's Advocate tries to DESTROY your position; Steelmanning strengthens the opposing one.

**Disconfirmation Search** (Klayman & Ha) — After forming a belief, deliberately search for evidence that would disprove it. Seek data you haven't looked at, people who disagree, cases where the pattern breaks. Best for fighting confirmation bias.

**Black Swan Hunting** (Taleb) — Identify the single observation that would destroy your entire thesis. Then actively look for it. Also: identify low-probability, high-impact events your plan is vulnerable to. Best for risk assessment and strategic planning.

**Stress Testing** — Subject your idea to extreme conditions and observe where it breaks. Test for worst plausible conditions, not normal ones. Best for systems design, financial planning, strategy robustness.

**Fermi Estimation as Falsification** (Fermi) — Estimate the rough magnitude of what your belief implies. If wildly inconsistent with observable reality, the belief is likely wrong. Best for quick sanity checks on claims and projections.

**Survivorship Audit** (Wald) — Before accepting a success pattern, identify who/what DIDN'T survive to be studied. Ask whether the pattern is real or an artifact of only looking at winners.

## 3. Analogical Reasoning

**Function:** GENERATE
**Core:** Recognize structural similarity between domains and import solutions from one to another.
**Unique power:** Accesses solutions invisible from inside your domain. The only operation that crosses domain boundaries.
**Blind spot:** Analogies can be seductive but structurally false. Must be followed by evaluation.
**Signature question:** "What field has already solved a structurally similar problem?"

### Moves

**Biomimicry** (Benyus) — Look for how nature solved the structural equivalent. Evolution has been running experiments for 3.8 billion years. Examples: Velcro from burrs, bullet train from kingfisher, building ventilation from termite mounds.

**Historical Analogy** (Neustadt & May) — Find a structurally similar historical situation. Critically: identify where the analogy BREAKS — the differences are often more important than the similarities.

**Cross-Industry Transfer** (Hargadon & Sutton) — Identify an industry with a structurally similar challenge and study their solution. Import the principle, not the specific mechanism.

**Metaphorical Mapping** (Lakoff & Johnson) — Use a metaphor as a structural bridge. Map relationships from one domain onto another and see what the mapping reveals. Every metaphor simultaneously illuminates and distorts — ask what it makes you miss.

**Distant Domain Import** (Dunbar, Gentner) — Deliberately search for parallels in the MOST DISTANT domains possible. Greater surface distance = more novel insight (when structural parallel holds). High reward, high risk.

**Isomorphic Transfer** — Identify a formal/mathematical structure that maps precisely onto your problem. Not loose analogy but exact structural correspondence. Best for network effects, game theory, information cascades.

**Functional Analogy** — Instead of "what looks like this?", ask "what DOES what this does?" Focus on function, not form. Two things that look completely different may serve the same function.

**Negative Analogy (Anti-Pattern Import)** — Find a domain where a structurally similar approach FAILED. Study the failure mechanism. Check whether your plan has the same structural vulnerability.

## 4. Abductive Reasoning

**Function:** GENERATE
**Core:** Given a surprising observation, generate the hypothesis that would make it non-surprising.
**Unique power:** Creates genuinely novel hypotheses with no precedent. The only operation that produces new explanatory frameworks.
**Blind spot:** Generates but doesn't verify. Must be followed by falsification or Bayesian evaluation.
**Signature question:** "What would have to be true to make this observation non-surprising?"

### Moves

**Anomaly Hunting** (Kuhn) — Scan for data points that DON'T FIT the current explanation. Anomalies are where new understanding hides. Best for when the current model "mostly works" but has unexplained residuals.

**Inference to Best Explanation (IBE)** (Peirce/Harman) — Generate multiple explanations, evaluate each against: explanatory scope, simplicity, consistency, predictive power, mechanism. Select the best. Limitation: "Best" is relative to generated candidates.

**Retroduction** (Peirce) — Work backward from observed result to possible rule and case. Result + Rule → Case. Always produces multiple competing hypotheses — feature, not bug.

**Diagnostic Reasoning** — Given symptoms, generate a differential diagnosis ranked by likelihood. Then identify the discriminating test between top candidates. Best for troubleshooting of any kind.

**Pattern Completion** — With a partial pattern, identify what the COMPLETE pattern would look like. What's missing? Best for market trend analysis and gap identification. Limitation: Brains sometimes complete patterns that aren't there (apophenia).

**Surprising Absence Detection** (Holmes) — Look for what SHOULD be present but ISN'T. The absence of an expected observation is itself a powerful data point. Best for fraud detection, competitive analysis, investigative reasoning.

**Abductive Cascade** — Generate a first-level explanation. Treat THAT as a new observation and ask "what would explain THIS?" Repeat. Each level produces deeper structural hypotheses. Limitation: Each level adds speculation — verify independently.

## 5. Counterfactual Analysis

**Function:** GENERATE
**Core:** Vary one condition while holding others constant to isolate structural contributions of specific factors.
**Unique power:** Performs controlled experiments in the mind. The only operation that teases apart what causes what.
**Blind spot:** Counterfactuals are imaginary. Quality depends entirely on your causal model.
**Signature question:** "What would change — and what wouldn't — if this one factor were different?"

### Moves

**Removal Test** — Imagine completely removing one factor. What collapses? What survives? Reveals actual contribution of any element.

**Sliding Scale** — Take a key variable, slide it from minimum to maximum. Find qualitative thresholds and tipping points. Best for identifying sensitivity and non-obvious thresholds.

**Temporal Counterfactual** (Ferguson) — Vary the TIMING rather than the event itself. Often the same event at a different time produces completely different outcomes. Most speculative of counterfactual moves.

**Substitution Test** — Replace one element with an alternative. Don't remove — replace. Reveals whether the specific element matters or any equivalent would do.

**Thought Experiment** (Einstein/Galileo) — Construct a simplified, idealized scenario isolating your variable. Doesn't need to be realistic — needs to be REVEALING.

**Parallel Universe Test** — Imagine 100 parallel versions making this decision. In how many does it work? Separates decision quality from outcome quality.

**Absence Counterfactual** — "What would NOT have happened without this?" Finds invisible contributions — the dog that didn't bark. Best for valuing prevention and maintenance.

**Regret Minimization Framework** (Bezos) — Project to age 80. Which scenario produces more regret? Specifically: which type of regret (action vs. inaction) is harder to live with?

**Ceteris Paribus Isolation** (Marshall) — Explicitly hold ALL other variables constant, vary only one. State assumptions out loud. Forces clarity about causal claims.

## 6. Dialectical Synthesis

**Function:** INTEGRATE
**Core:** Hold opposing positions simultaneously. Allow tension to produce higher-order insight transcending both.
**Unique power:** Produces insights NEITHER position contains alone. Not compromise — genuine synthesis.
**Blind spot:** Can get trapped in endless dialectics without conclusions. Needs a stopping point.
**Signature question:** "What becomes visible ONLY when I take both sides seriously at the same time?"

### Moves

**Hegelian Triad** — Thesis → Antithesis → Synthesis. The synthesis preserves truth in both while resolving the contradiction. Limitation: Can produce superficial synthesis that acknowledges tension without transcending it.

**Both/And Reframe** (Johnson/Collins) — Ask: "Is this a polarity to manage rather than a problem to solve?" If yes, the goal is wise oscillation between poles, not choosing one.

**Polarity Mapping** (Johnson) — For any tension: identify upside of each pole, downside of each pole. Over-focusing on one pole's upside eventually produces the other's downside. Map the cycle to prevent reactive swinging.

**Paradox Integration** (Smith & Lewis) — Identify genuine paradoxes — both true AND contradictory. Don't resolve — learn to operate within the tension. Requires tolerance for ambiguity.

**Dialectical Negation** (Hegel) — Don't just negate a position (say "no"). Perform DETERMINATE negation — identify what SPECIFICALLY is wrong. Let that specific wrongness generate its own specific correction.

**Constructive Disagreement Protocol** — Two sides must: present strongest case, listen, identify what's TRUE in the opposing case, then attempt synthesis. Requires good faith.

**Steel Dialectics** — Steelman BOTH sides before synthesizing. Build each position maximally strong, then synthesize from strongest versions. Time-intensive but produces the most robust synthesis.

## 7. Systems Thinking

**Function:** INTEGRATE
**Core:** Map relationships, feedback loops, delays, and emergent properties. See how parts interact to produce wholes.
**Unique power:** Reveals unintended consequences and counterintuitive dynamics from interconnection.
**Blind spot:** Can see everything connecting to everything without prioritizing. Tends toward holism that resists decomposition.
**Signature question:** "What does this connect to that nobody is tracking, and what feedback loops are operating invisibly?"

### Moves

**Causal Loop Diagramming** (Forrester/Meadows) — Draw relationships as arrows. Label as positive (same direction) or negative (opposite). Identify reinforcing loops (amplify) and balancing loops (stabilize). The system's behavior depends on which loop dominates.

**Stock and Flow Analysis** (Forrester) — Distinguish STOCKS (accumulations, like a bathtub) from FLOWS (rates, like faucets and drains). Most people confuse these, leading to systematic errors.

**Leverage Point Analysis** (Meadows) — Find where small intervention produces largest change. Ranked weakest to strongest: numbers → buffer sizes → structure → delays → negative feedback → positive feedback → information flows → rules → power to change rules → goals → paradigm → power to transcend paradigms.

**Boundary Analysis** (Ulrich) — Examine where you've drawn the system boundary. What's excluded? Whose perspectives are missing? Redrawing the boundary changes what solutions are visible.

**Emergence Scanning** (Holland/Kauffman) — Look for properties at the system level that don't exist at the component level. Culture isn't in any single person. Market price isn't in any single buyer.

**Delay Mapping** (Senge) — Identify time delays between actions and consequences. Short delays = stable systems. Long delays = oscillation and overreaction.

**Archetypes Recognition** (Senge) — Match behavior to common archetypes: Shifting the Burden, Limits to Growth, Tragedy of the Commons, Fixes that Fail, Escalation, Success to the Successful, Growth and Underinvestment. If you recognize the archetype, you can predict the trajectory.

**Unintended Consequences Tracing** (Merton) — For any intervention: trace first-order intended effects, first-order unintended effects, second-order effects of both, effects on adjacent systems. Ask: who will change their behavior in response, and what will THAT cause?

## 8. Bayesian Updating

**Function:** EVALUATE
**Core:** Assign explicit probabilities and update them systematically as evidence arrives.
**Unique power:** Prevents both overconfidence and underconfidence. Continuously calibrated confidence.
**Blind spot:** Requires a prior, and choosing the prior is subjective. Struggles with unprecedented events.
**Signature question:** "Given this new evidence, precisely how much should my confidence change?"

### Moves

**Base Rate Check** (Kahneman & Tversky) — Before evaluating your specific case, find how often this type of thing happens in general. Use as your starting point, then adjust for specifics.

**Likelihood Ratio Assessment** — For evidence, ask: "How likely if hypothesis TRUE? How likely if FALSE?" The ratio tells you how much to update. High ratio = strong evidence. Low ratio = barely informative.

**Confidence Calibration** (Tetlock) — State belief AND numerical confidence. Track over time: of things you said 70% confident about, were ~70% true? Identifies systematic over/underconfidence.

**Evidence Weighting** — Assess quality before updating. Hierarchy (weakest to strongest): anecdote → expert opinion → case study → observational study → controlled experiment → systematic review → meta-analysis.

**Prior Elicitation** — Before encountering evidence, make your prior belief EXPLICIT in writing. Prevents hindsight bias ("I knew it all along"). Makes it possible to track actual updating.

**Update Bracketing** — Before new evidence, define in advance how much different types should move you. Prevents emotional overreaction to vivid but low-quality evidence.

**Probability Decomposition** — Break complex probabilities into easier-to-estimate components. Then combine. Often reveals combined probability is much lower than gut feeling. Limitation: Assumes independence between components.

**Adversarial Collaboration** (Kahneman) — Find someone who disagrees. Together design a test whose outcome both agree would move beliefs. Commit to the update in advance. Then run the test.

## 9. Perspective Simulation

**Function:** INTEGRATE
**Core:** Model what others know, believe, want, intend, and experience with enough fidelity that they'd recognize the simulation as their own.
**Unique power:** Forces genuine engagement with perspectives your mind instinctively dismisses. The constraint of fidelity distinguishes this from casual "seeing the other side."
**Blind spot:** Can lead to excessive epistemic humility. Not every position deserves maximally charitable treatment.
**Signature question:** "What would the smartest, most informed advocate of this position say — and what do they see that I'm missing?"

### Moves

**Ideological Turing Test** (Caplan) — State the opposing position so accurately an advocate couldn't tell you disagree. If a true believer would endorse your summary, you've passed.

**Full Role Entry** — Temporarily BECOME a person who holds the position. Adopt their values, constraints, experiences. Ask: given everything they've experienced, is their position actually rational?

**Strongest Possible Objection** — Against your OWN position, formulate the single most devastating objection. Not the most common — the one that would destroy your position entirely if true.

**Charitable Interpretation** (Quine/Davidson) — When interpreting someone's statement, choose the interpretation that makes them MOST rational, not least. Default, not absolute rule.

**Taboo Your Words** (Yudkowsky) — Forbid the key term in the disagreement. Explain both positions using only concrete language. Often reveals the disagreement is about word meaning, not reality.

**Steelman the Data** — When data contradicts your position, don't look for flaws first. Build the strongest case that THE DATA IS TELLING THE TRUTH and you're wrong. What would the world look like if the data is right?

**Stakeholder Voice Simulation** — For each stakeholder affected by a decision, simulate their perspective at full fidelity. What would they say if in the room? What do they know that you don't?

**Opposite Expertise Test** — Find the most credentialed expert who DISAGREES with you. Study their argument. The question isn't "are they right?" but "what do they know that leads them to a different conclusion?"
