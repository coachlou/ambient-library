# Extraction Dimensions Reference

The eight dimensions the Cognitive Mirror observes. Each dimension includes what
to look for, what counts as evidence, and common pitfalls in observation.

---

## 1. Mode Detection

**What to observe:** Which operational mode is active? What triggered it? Is the
user blending modes (e.g., architecture thinking applied to a content problem)?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Task type is the primary signal (building infrastructure → Architecture mode;
  developing a talk → Presentation mode; naming a methodology → Framework mode;
  analyzing audience/market → Strategy mode)
- Mode blending shows up as vocabulary crossing (using systems architecture
  language in a content conversation, or IP-protection framing in a technical
  discussion)
- Mode transitions often happen mid-conversation — track the pivot point

**Pitfall:** Don't force a single mode onto a complex conversation. Many sessions
genuinely span multiple modes. Note the transitions and what triggered them.

---

## 2. Dominant Cognitive Operation

**What to observe:** Which of the 9 cognitive operations (from the Cognitive
Operations Architecture) is driving the user's thinking?

**Evidence criteria:**
- First Principles → breaking things down to primitives, questioning assumptions
- Falsification → looking for what would disprove a claim, stress-testing
- Abduction → naming patterns, creating frameworks from observations
- Analogical Reasoning → cross-domain metaphors, "this is like..."
- Dialectical Synthesis → combining opposing ideas into something new
- Perspective Simulation → "what would they think/feel/do?"
- Probabilistic → weighing likelihoods, hedging, uncertainty language
- Temporal → sequencing, dependencies, "what happens after X?"
- Counterfactual → "what if we hadn't done X?" alternative histories

**Pitfall:** Don't confuse the topic with the operation. A conversation about
probability doesn't mean Probabilistic Reasoning is dominant — the user might
be applying First Principles thinking TO a probability topic.

---

## 3. Quality Gates

**What to observe:** What did the user accept? What did they reject? What was
the criterion for rejection?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Explicit rejections: "no," "that's not right," "try again"
- Calibration language: "too much," "not enough," "wrong level"
- Implicit rejections: user ignores output and redirects
- Acceptance signals: "yes," "good," builds on the output, moves forward
- The most revealing gates are rejections with stated reasons

**Pitfall:** Not every redirect is a quality rejection. Sometimes the user is
exploring, not evaluating. Look for evaluative language specifically.

This is the highest-signal dimension. Rejections reveal standards more clearly
than acceptances.

---

## 4. Decision Heuristics

**What to observe:** When faced with a tradeoff, what did the user optimize for?
What did they sacrifice? What's the implicit priority hierarchy?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Explicit tradeoff language: "I'd rather X than Y"
- What gets cut when scope must shrink (reveals what's not essential)
- What gets added even when scope should shrink (reveals what IS essential)
- Speed vs. quality choices
- Pragmatic vs. principled choices
- Simple vs. comprehensive choices

**Pitfall:** Don't infer priorities from a single decision. Track across
multiple conversations to see if the pattern holds.

---

## 5. Interaction Choreography

**What to observe:** How does the user structure the work? How do they signal
direction changes?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Specification style: upfront spec vs. iterative discovery
- Reaction pattern: reacts to artifacts vs. reacts to descriptions
- Direction signals: explicit ("do X next") vs. implicit (shifts topic)
- Correction style: precise edit vs. general direction change
- Engagement signals: long responses = deep engagement; short responses = steering

**Pitfall:** Don't confuse platform constraints with user preferences. Short
mobile responses don't mean the user prefers brevity.

---

## 6. Energy Markers

**What to observe:** What gets deep engagement vs. what goes flat?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Length and detail of user responses (longer = more engaged)
- Follow-up questions (asking more = interested)
- Building on output vs. just acknowledging it
- Returning to a topic after moving away
- Emotional language or emphasis (exclamation marks, strong language)

**Pitfall:** Low energy doesn't always mean disinterest. Sometimes it means
satisfaction — the output was right and there's nothing to add. Look for the
combination of low energy AND topic change.

---

## 7. Framework Instinct

**What to observe:** When does the user reach for structure? When do they resist
it? What kind of structure do they build?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Spontaneous naming of patterns ("let's call this the X model")
- Structural language: "there are three layers," "the first step is"
- Resistance to premature structure: "don't formalize this yet"
- Structure type preferences: numbered steps vs. named components vs.
  spatial metaphors (layers, maps, spectrums)

**Pitfall:** Don't confuse requesting structure from Claude with the user's
own structural instinct. The user asking "can you organize this?" is different
from the user spontaneously organizing.

---

## 8. Absent Signals

**What to observe:** What expected patterns didn't appear?

**Evidence criteria:**
- Patterns documented in the profile that weren't observed
- Modes that should have activated but didn't
- Quality gates that weren't applied in a context where they usually are
- Cognitive operations that are usually dominant but were absent

**Pitfall:** Absence is weaker evidence than presence. A pattern not appearing
in one conversation doesn't mean it's fading — it might just not have been
relevant. Track absence across multiple conversations before flagging.
