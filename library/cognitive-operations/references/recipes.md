## STRATEGIC DECISION-MAKING

### Recipe 1: The Wrong-Problem Detector

**Use when:** Progress has stalled despite increasingly sophisticated analysis. You suspect you might be solving the wrong problem.
**NOT for:** Well-defined problems with steady progress. Execution bottlenecks, not framing ones. Simpler heuristic: "What's the next concrete action?"

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Zeroth Principle, lens: Inversion. Question assumptions about what the problem IS, from the direction of failure.
2. Abductive Reasoning → Anomaly Hunting, lens: Inversion. If the problem ISN'T what you think, what alternative problem would make your failure pattern non-surprising?
3. Counterfactual → Removal Test, lens: Chesterton's Fence. If the blockage were removed, would you progress — or does it serve a function?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment. Design a test: what would you observe if the new framing is correct vs. incorrect?

**Abandon when surprised:** If genuine surprise emerges at any step, stop — the surprise IS the insight.
**Unique:** Only recipe that attacks the problem DEFINITION rather than the problem itself.

### Recipe 2: The Decision Clarifier

**Use when:** Consequential choice with incomplete information and time to think.
**NOT for:** Easily reversible decisions. Trusted expert available. Simpler heuristic: "One-way or two-way door?"

**Steps:**
1. Bayesian → Base Rate Check, lens: Survivorship Bias. What do base rates say? Am I only seeing successes?
2. Perspective Simulation → Steelmanning, lens: Inversion. Strongest case for my least-preferred option, from the angle of what could go wrong with my preferred one.
3. Counterfactual → Regret Minimization, lens: Temporal Discounting. In 10 years, which regret is worse — and which type am I underweighting?
4. First Principles → Assumption Mapping. What's confirmed, unconfirmed, and critical?

**Abandon when surprised:** If base rates are far different from your assumption, recalibrate before continuing.
**Unique:** Sequences four evaluation types — statistical, adversarial, temporal, structural.

---

### Recipe 3: The Bet Sizer

**Use when:** You know WHAT to do but not how much to commit.
**NOT for:** Trivially small or reversible commitments. Simpler heuristic: "Smallest bet that gives meaningful information?"

**Steps:**
1. Bayesian → Confidence Calibration, lens: Asymmetric Upside. State confidence as percentage. Map payoff structure.
2. Counterfactual → Sliding Scale, lens: Diminishing Returns. Slide commitment min to max. Where does 80% of value come? Where do additional resources stop producing?
3. Falsification → Pre-Mortem, lens: Fat Tails. If it fails, what's maximum damage? Bounded or cascading? Risking ruin?
4. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Optionality. Structure for staged commitment with early signals.

**Abandon when surprised:** If Pre-Mortem reveals ruin scenario, stop. No expected value justifies bets that can destroy you.
**Unique:** Only recipe for calibrating HOW MUCH, not WHAT.

### Recipe 4: The Pivot Evaluator

**Use when:** Torn between doubling down and changing direction. Both have genuine merit. Oscillating for weeks.
**NOT for:** Haven't given current path enough time. Pivot driven by boredom not evidence. Simpler heuristic: "Has the current approach had a fair test?"

**Steps:**
1. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Sunk Cost Awareness. What evidence would conclusively show the current path is structurally dead?
2. Analogical Reasoning → Historical Analogy, lens: S-Curves. Where on the S-curve am I? Painful start or peaked flat top?
3. Counterfactual → Substitution Test, lens: Opportunity Cost. What would someone with zero emotional attachment choose?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Both/And Reframe. Is there a version that preserves best of both?

**Abandon when surprised:** If you cannot define what failure would look like for the current path, you're protecting an identity. Switch to Recipe 36.
**Unique:** Addresses four reasons pivots get stuck: unclear failure criteria, phase misidentification, sunk cost attachment, false binaries.

---

### Recipe 5: The Timing Optimizer

**Use when:** Right move, uncertain timing. Too early is as costly as too late.
**NOT for:** When delay cost clearly exceeds premature action cost. Simpler heuristic: "Best time was 20 years ago. Second best is now."

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Delay Mapping, lens: Phase Transitions. Key delays? How long from action to result? Phase transition points?
2. Bayesian → Base Rate Check, lens: Planning Fallacy. Similar timing decisions — how many moved too early vs. too late?
3. Counterfactual → Temporal Counterfactual, lens: Path Dependence. Trace consequences of acting now vs. 3 months vs. 12 months. Path-dependent lockouts?
4. Abductive → Surprising Absence Detection, lens: Signal vs. Noise. What leading indicators to watch? What signal should be present NOW if timing is right?

**Abandon when surprised:** If delays are much longer than assumed, recalculate immediately.

---

### Recipe 6: The Exit Strategist

**Use when:** You suspect you should quit but can't tell if it's wisdom or weakness.
**NOT for:** Haven't genuinely committed yet. Difficulty is temporary and predictable. Simpler heuristic: "Dip or dead end?" (Godin)

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Assumption Mapping, lens: Sunk Cost Awareness. List every reason you're still in. Kill every backward-looking reason. What's left?
2. Perspective Simulation → Strongest Possible Objection, lens: Revealed Preferences. Strongest case for staying. Does your behavior match it?
3. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Reversibility. Can you test the exit — trial period, sabbatical, pause?
4. Counterfactual → Regret Minimization, lens: Temporal Discounting. At 80, which haunts more — staying too long or leaving too soon?

**Abandon when surprised:** If every reason for staying is backward-looking, you have your answer.

## INNOVATION & CREATION

### Recipe 7: The Innovation Engine

**Use when:** Need genuinely novel ideas, not recombination. Exhausted obvious approaches.
**NOT for:** Conventional approach untried. Execution speed is the constraint. Simpler heuristic: "What's worked in similar situations?"

**Steps:**
1. Analogical Reasoning → Distant Domain Import, lens: far-transfer domains. Parallels in biology, music, urban planning.
2. First Principles → Regressive Abstraction. Strip most promising analogy to structural core principle.
3. Dialectical Synthesis → Both/And Reframe, lens: real-world constraints. What emerges when abstract insight meets concrete limitations?
4. Falsification → Pre-Mortem. If implemented and failed spectacularly, what went wrong?

**Abandon when surprised:** If an analogy triggers an unexpected connection, follow that thread first.

---

### Recipe 8: The Knowledge Creation Engine

**Use when:** Want to produce genuinely new understanding, not retrieve existing knowledge.
**NOT for:** Existing knowledge suffices. Retrieval and synthesis are what's needed.

**Steps (RECURSIVE — feed output back to Step 1):**
1. First Principles → Zeroth Principle. Decompose to deepest foundations.
2. Analogical Reasoning → Distant Domain Import (maximum distance).
3. Dialectical Synthesis → Determinate Negation. Hold import in tension with what's known. Let tension produce something new.
4. Falsification → Disconfirmation Search. Attack what emerged. What survives?
5. Feed survivors back to Step 1. Run 4 cycles.

**Abandon when surprised:** If Disconfirmation destroys your idea unexpectedly, study HOW it broke — the destruction pattern may contain the breakthrough.
**Unique:** Only recursive recipe. This procedure produced the Cognitive Operations Architecture itself.

---

### Recipe 9: The Category Creator

**Use when:** Crowded space, incremental improvement won't work. Need to redefine the game.
**NOT for:** Haven't mastered existing category. Lack resources to educate market. Simpler heuristic: "Win the existing game first."

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Zeroth Principle, lens: Frame Control. What fundamental NEED does this category serve? Who defined it and what assumptions did they embed?
2. Perspective Simulation → Full Role Entry, lens: Curse of Knowledge. Become the customer who doesn't know this category exists. How do they describe their need?
3. Analogical Reasoning → Cross-Industry Transfer, lens: Creative Destruction. Industries that successfully redefined their category — import the mechanism.
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Determinate Negation, lens: Blue Ocean. Name what the existing category gets specifically wrong. New category emerges from that wrongness.
5. Falsification → Stress Testing. If adopted, who fights it? Do objections reveal genuine problems or incumbent resistance?

**Abandon when surprised:** If customers already describe their need in language that maps to no existing category, you've found the gap.

---

### Recipe 10: The Constraint Alchemist

**Use when:** A limitation everyone treats as fixed might be an advantage in disguise.
**NOT for:** Constraint is genuinely immovable. Simpler heuristic: "Accept it and optimize around it."

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Physics-Style Reasoning, lens: Map vs. Territory. Is this actually fixed or conventional? "We can't" vs. "nobody has."
2. Analogical Reasoning → Negative Analogy, lens: Antifragility. Who turned this same constraint into advantage? Import mechanism.
3. Counterfactual → Removal Test, lens: Chesterton's Fence. If constraint disappeared, would you actually be better off?
4. Abductive → Pattern Completion, lens: Asymmetric Upside. What innovation is ONLY possible because of this constraint?

**Abandon when surprised:** If the Removal Test shows the constraint produces significant hidden value, you want to amplify it, not overcome it.

---

### Recipe 11: The Paradigm Breaker

**Use when:** Conventional wisdom feels increasingly wrong but you can't articulate why.
**NOT for:** Lack expertise to distinguish paradigm problem from knowledge gap.

**Steps:**
1. Abductive → Anomaly Hunting, lens: Punctuated Equilibrium. Catalogue anomalies the current paradigm keeps explaining away.
2. First Principles → Cartesian Doubt, lens: Legibility. Doubt everything — definitions, categories, metrics, implicit ontology.
3. Analogical Reasoning → Historical Analogy, lens: Creative Destruction. Find historical paradigm shifts. Where in the pattern is your field?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Determinate Negation. Name what the current paradigm is specifically wrong about. Let that generate its correction.
5. Falsification → Black Swan Hunting. What single observation would kill the emerging new paradigm?

**Unique:** Most ambitious recipe. Follows the actual structure of Kuhnian paradigm shifts, compressed into deliberate procedure.

## DIAGNOSIS & PROBLEM-SOLVING

### Recipe 12: The Blind Spot Finder

**Use when:** Metrics look fine but gut says something's off. Others see something you can't.
**NOT for:** Problem is clear, path obvious. Simpler heuristic: "What's the one thing I'm avoiding?"

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Unintended Consequences Tracing, lens: Second-Order Effects.
2. Perspective Simulation → Strongest Possible Objection, lens: shadow perspectives. Build the case for the perspective you're most allergic to.
3. Abductive → Surprising Absence Detection, lens: Goodhart's Law. Are metrics measuring what matters? What should be present but isn't?
4. Analogical Reasoning → Negative Analogy, lens: Historical Precedent.

**Abandon when surprised:** If the Strongest Objection generates genuine discomfort you want to argue against, that defensive reaction IS the signal.

---

### Recipe 13: The Root Cause Excavator

**Use when:** Same problem keeps returning despite repeated fixes.
**NOT for:** Genuinely new problem. Recurrence due to poor execution of known fix.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Five Whys then Zeroth Principle, lens: Shifting Baseline Syndrome. Root cause of the root cause. Has baseline shifted?
2. Abductive → Abductive Cascade, lens: Feedback Loops. Why does it keep coming back? Self-reinforcing loops?
3. Systems Thinking → Archetypes Recognition, lens: Iatrogenics. Shifting the Burden? Fixes that Fail? Are your interventions making it worse?
4. Counterfactual → Absence Counterfactual, lens: Chesterton's Fence. Does the recurring problem serve a hidden function?

**Abandon when surprised:** If your previous fixes ARE the mechanism of recurrence — stop fixing, start redesigning.

---

### Recipe 14: The Stagnation Breaker

**Use when:** Plateau. Working harder, results flat. What got you here stopped working.
**NOT for:** Natural dip phase. External factors you can't control.

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Archetypes Recognition, lens: Limits to Growth. Which constraint became binding at this level?
2. First Principles → Regressive Abstraction, lens: Local vs. Global Optimum. Am I at a local optimum? Different approach, same principle, higher peak?
3. Analogical Reasoning → Cross-Industry Transfer, lens: S-Curves. Fields that broke similar plateaus — what was the breakthrough mechanism?
4. Counterfactual → Thought Experiment, lens: Adaptive Valley. Starting from zero today with everything I know — what would I build?

**Abandon when surprised:** If the binding constraint is YOU rather than the strategy, switch to Recipe 37 (Growth Edge Finder).

---

### Recipe 15: The Complexity Reducer

**Use when:** Situation so tangled nobody can see clearly enough to act. Paralysis by analysis.
**NOT for:** Complexity is genuine and irreducible. Simpler heuristic: "Act despite complexity."

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Via Negativa, lens: Signal vs. Noise. Subtract confusion. Which variables are noise? Which concerns are duplicates?
2. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Pareto. Of what remains, which 20% drives 80%?
3. Counterfactual → Removal Test, lens: McNamara Fallacy. For each element, if removed, would core problem change?
4. Analogical Reasoning → Metaphorical Mapping. Find a simple metaphor capturing essential structure.

**Abandon when surprised:** If Via Negativa strips >70% of what you were tracking, you were managing complexity theater.
**Unique:** Only recipe that works primarily by SUBTRACTION.

---

### Recipe 16: The Pattern Interrupt

**Use when:** Same failure pattern despite being fully aware of it. Awareness hasn't broken the cycle.
**NOT for:** First time the pattern appeared. Pattern is functional.

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Causal Loop Diagramming, lens: Homeostasis. Map the loop including the hidden BENEFIT the pattern provides.
2. Abductive → Diagnostic Reasoning, lens: Revealed Preferences. What problem is the dysfunctional behavior solving?
3. Counterfactual → Substitution Test, lens: Functional Analogy. What alternative meets the same hidden need without destructive effects?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment. Smallest experiment testing whether the substitute satisfies the hidden need.

**Abandon when surprised:** If the hidden benefit is identity protection, switch to Recipe 36 (Identity Audit).
**Unique:** Treats persistent patterns as functional, not irrational. "Bad" habits as solutions to unnamed problems.

## RISK & UNCERTAINTY

### Recipe 17: Black Swan Preparedness Protocol

**Use when:** Need to prepare for unpredictable catastrophic events. Plans assume narrow "normal."
**NOT for:** Well-understood, quantifiable risks. Standard hedging available.

**Steps:**
1. Falsification → Black Swan Hunting, lens: Fat Tails. For each assumption, what impossible-seeming event would violate it catastrophically?
2. Systems Thinking → Unintended Consequences Tracing, lens: Butterfly Effect. For each Swan, trace the cascade.
3. Counterfactual → Parallel Universe Test, lens: Antifragility. Across 100 futures, how many include a tail event? Does your structure survive, break, or benefit?
4. First Principles → Via Negativa, lens: Asymmetric Upside. Remove fragilities. Eliminate single points of failure.

**Abandon when surprised:** If a specific Black Swan has actual leading indicators already present, switch to specific contingency planning.
**Unique:** Abandons prediction entirely. Builds robustness for irreducible uncertainty.

---

### Recipe 18: The Uncertainty Navigator

**Use when:** Must decide now, needed information won't arrive in time.
**NOT for:** Can wait for info at low cost. Decision is reversible.

**Steps:**
1. Bayesian → Probability Decomposition, lens: Base Rate Neglect. Break into component probabilities. Compare combined estimate to gut feeling.
2. Counterfactual → Ceteris Paribus Isolation, lens: Diminishing Returns. Which uncertain variable actually matters? Slide it — at what point does decision change?
3. Falsification → Stress Testing, lens: Reversibility. Test preferred option against worst plausible case. Survivable?
4. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Optionality. Structure for maximum optionality — commit direction, keep path adjustable.

**Abandon when surprised:** If component probabilities are more favorable than gut suggests, you may be suffering from ambiguity aversion.

---

### Recipe 19: The Downside Limiter

**Use when:** Attractive upside, potentially fatal downside. Need asymmetric positioning.
**NOT for:** Both upside and downside are modest.

**Steps:**
1. Falsification → Pre-Mortem, lens: Fat Tails. Map every failure mode. Classify: bounded or unbounded downside?
2. Systems Thinking → Causal Loop Diagramming, lens: Moral Hazard. How do losses cascade? Feedback loops that amplify? Gambler's trap incentives?
3. Counterfactual → Sliding Scale, lens: Asymmetric Upside. Slide exposure min to max. Sweet spot for upside/downside ratio?
4. First Principles → Assumption Mapping, lens: Skin in the Game. Are upside conditions and worst-case conditions correlated?

**Abandon when surprised:** If unbounded downside can't be structured away — don't play, regardless of upside.

---

### Recipe 20: The Antifragility Designer

**Use when:** Want to build something that benefits from volatility and stress.
**NOT for:** Stable, predictable environment. Low stakes.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Physics-Style Reasoning, lens: Antifragility. Mechanisms: overcompensation, redundancy, optionality, information gain. Which are available?
2. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Feedback Loops. Where to convert stressors into information? Build overcompensation? Replace large bets with small ones?
3. Falsification → Stress Testing, lens: Via Negativa. Before adding antifragile features, remove fragilities. Where does it break first?
4. Analogical Reasoning → Biomimicry, lens: Natural Selection. Import biological antifragility mechanism.
5. Counterfactual → Thought Experiment. Major shock next month — walk through what happens in the redesigned system.

**Abandon when surprised:** If system breaks at much lower stress than expected, you need basic robustness first. Switch to Recipe 17.

## COMMUNICATION & PERSUASION

### Recipe 21: The Argument Strengthener

**Use when:** Position keeps failing to persuade despite multiple attempts.
**NOT for:** The idea itself is wrong. Haven't reached the right audience.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Ideological Turing Test, lens: Curse of Knowledge. State opposing position accurately enough an opponent would endorse it.
2. Falsification → Strongest Possible Objection, lens: Confirmation Bias. Single most devastating objection. Address head-on.
3. Abductive → Diagnostic Reasoning, lens: Narrative Fallacy. Why is the argument failing? Too abstract? Wrong concern? List vs. story?
4. Analogical Reasoning → Metaphorical Mapping, lens: Anchoring. The metaphor that makes the insight land in 10 seconds.

---

### Recipe 22: The Audience Translator

**Use when:** Deep understanding that produces blank stares from your audience.
**NOT for:** Audience doesn't need to understand, just trust. Simpler message would suffice.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Full Role Entry, lens: Curse of Knowledge. Become your audience. Their frameworks, language, problems.
2. First Principles → Regressive Abstraction, lens: Map vs. Territory. Core principle in one sentence a 12-year-old would understand.
3. Analogical Reasoning → Functional Analogy, lens: Anchoring. What does the audience already understand that WORKS LIKE this?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Both/And Reframe, lens: Commitment and Consistency Bias. Show how insight EXTENDS what they already believe.

---

### Recipe 23: The Objection Anticipator

**Use when:** Presenting to a critical audience. Need to preempt strongest counterarguments.
**NOT for:** Audience already on your side.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Stakeholder Voice Simulation, lens: Principal-Agent Problem. Each person's incentives, fears, what they need to protect.
2. Falsification → Devil's Advocate, lens: Status Games. Most aggressive PRIVATE objections, including political ones.
3. Bayesian → Evidence Weighting, lens: Anchoring. Most persuasive evidence for EACH specific person's epistemic style.
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Steel Dialectics. Steelman strongest objections, acknowledge what's valid, show how proposal incorporates the concern.

---

### Recipe 24: The Narrative Constructor

**Use when:** Data/evidence but no compelling story. Material convinces on merits but fails to engage.
**NOT for:** Purely analytical audience where a structured argument suffices.

**Steps:**
1. Abductive → Pattern Completion, lens: Narrative Fallacy. The underlying story: protagonist, conflict, turning point, implication.
2. Perspective Simulation → Full Role Entry, lens: Peak-End Rule. Design emotional journey. Peak moment and closing beat.
3. Analogical Reasoning → Metaphorical Mapping, lens: Anchoring. Single metaphor that carries the whole story.
4. Falsification → Devil's Advocate, lens: Survivorship Bias. What contradictory data has been edited away?

## LEARNING & UNDERSTANDING

### Recipe 25: The Mental Model Extractor

**Use when:** An expert does something brilliantly but can't explain how.

**Steps:**
1. Abductive → Diagnostic Reasoning, lens: Curse of Knowledge. Observe decisions a novice wouldn't make. Infer model from behavior.
2. First Principles → Regressive Abstraction. "What is this decision an instance of?" — find the principle explaining multiple decisions.
3. Counterfactual → Substitution Test, lens: Comparative Advantage. If a novice followed this principle, would they look expert? Gap = what's missing.
4. Analogical Reasoning → Isomorphic Transfer, lens: Pattern Recognition. Does the extracted model map onto any known framework?
5. Falsification → Crucial Experiment. Find a case where the model predicts one thing and common wisdom predicts another. Check against expert intuition.

---

### Recipe 26: The Expertise Accelerator

**Use when:** Need functional competence in a new domain fast.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Assumption Mapping, lens: Pareto. The 20% of concepts generating 80% of explanatory power.
2. Analogical Reasoning → Cross-Industry Transfer, lens: Functional Analogy. What from other domains transfers structurally?
3. Perspective Simulation → Opposite Expertise Test, lens: Dunning-Kruger. What do best practitioners think beginners get most conceptually wrong?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Circle of Competence. Test understanding against real problems. Where does framework break?

**Abandon when surprised:** If NOTHING transfers cleanly, slow down. Domain resists analogical learning.

---

### Recipe 27: The Assumption Archaeologist

**Use when:** Suspect deep unexamined assumptions constrain thinking but can't identify them.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Cartesian Doubt, lens: Legibility. Doubt everything — definitions, categories, metrics, implicit ontology.
2. Abductive → Surprising Absence Detection, lens: Streetlight Effect. What questions is nobody asking? What data is nobody collecting?
3. Analogical Reasoning → Historical Analogy, lens: Shifting Baseline Syndrome. What did practitioners believe 50 years ago that they've abandoned? What will they abandon next?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Paradox Integration, lens: Map vs. Territory. Hold your deepest assumption and its negation simultaneously.

**Abandon when surprised:** If Cartesian Doubt produces genuine vertigo, stay with it. You've hit an assumption that matters.

---

### Recipe 28: The Transfer Engine

**Use when:** Hard-won expertise in one domain, want to apply its deep structure to a new one.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Regressive Abstraction. Abstract your expertise repeatedly to find meta-principles.
2. Analogical Reasoning → Isomorphic Transfer, lens: Functional Analogy. Map formal structure onto new domain. Where does mapping hold and break?
3. Falsification → Disconfirmation Search, lens: Halo Effect. Where does the transfer NOT work? Where does existing intuition mislead?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Determinate Negation. Hold failures and successes together. Create hybrid model adapted to new domain.

## EXECUTION & IMPLEMENTATION

### Recipe 29: The Bottleneck Finder

**Use when:** High effort, low throughput. System is busy but not productive.

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Stock and Flow Analysis, lens: Pareto. Where does work accumulate? Follow the pile-up.
2. First Principles → Five Whys, lens: Goodhart's Law. Why 5x at the accumulation. Are metrics tracking the right thing?
3. Counterfactual → Removal Test, lens: Diminishing Returns. If you doubled capacity here, would total throughput change?
4. Abductive → Surprising Absence Detection, lens: Iatrogenics. Is a previous "solution" itself the bottleneck?

---

### Recipe 30: The Minimum Viable Path

**Use when:** Overwhelmed, every plan version requires more than you have.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Via Negativa, lens: Pareto. Core objective — subtract everything that doesn't serve it.
2. Counterfactual → Removal Test, lens: Reversibility. For each remaining element, still achievable without it? Removable = remove.
3. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Gall's Law. Of minimum set, which element has most leverage?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Critical Mass. Smallest experiment testing whether minimal version works.

**Abandon when surprised:** If Removal Test reduces the plan to something embarrassingly simple, that's probably right.

---

### Recipe 31: The Unintended Consequences Scanner

**Use when:** About to implement major change. Need to anticipate cascading effects.

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Unintended Consequences Tracing, lens: Second-Order Effects. First, second, third-order effects. Who changes behavior in response?
2. Perspective Simulation → Stakeholder Voice Simulation, lens: Cobra Effect. Each stakeholder's self-interested response. Where will people game it?
3. Analogical Reasoning → Negative Analogy, lens: Hysteresis. Historical cases with surprising consequences. Are any negative consequences irreversible?
4. Counterfactual → Thought Experiment, lens: Iatrogenics. 12 months later — what NEW problems exist that didn't before?

---

### Recipe 32: The Implementation Stress Test

**Use when:** Plan looks good on paper, nagging sense something breaks in execution.

**Steps:**
1. Falsification → Pre-Mortem, lens: Planning Fallacy. 12 months from now, it failed. Write the postmortem. 10 specific failure scenes.
2. Counterfactual → Ceteris Paribus Isolation, lens: Sensitivity Analysis. For each failure mode, how sensitive? Which ones break the plan vs. dent it?
3. Systems Thinking → Delay Mapping, lens: Compounding. Every delay in the timeline. Where does a small delay cascade catastrophically?
4. Perspective Simulation → Full Role Entry, lens: Execution Gap. Become each person responsible. From THEIR perspective, is this step actually executable on time?

## RELATIONSHIPS & NEGOTIATION

### Recipe 33: The Negotiation Mapper

**Use when:** High-stakes negotiation. Need full landscape before you begin.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Full Role Entry, lens: Asymmetric Information. What does the other party want (not stated position)? Their BATNA?
2. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Principal-Agent Problem. Full incentive landscape. Personal incentives of decision-makers?
3. Counterfactual → Substitution Test, lens: Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum. Can we create value? Different deal structure?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Both/And Reframe, lens: Optionality. Three creative structures giving both parties MORE than expected.

---

### Recipe 34: The Conflict Resolver

**Use when:** Two parties locked in opposition. Compromise already attempted and rejected.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Ideological Turing Test, lens: Revealed Preferences. State each side's position accurately enough they'd endorse it. Does behavior match?
2. First Principles → Zeroth Principle, lens: Frame Control. Are they actually disagreeing about what they think? Reframe the conflict.
3. Abductive → Surprising Absence Detection, lens: Mimetic Desire. What needs are NOT expressed? Hidden status, respect, autonomy, fear?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Determinate Negation, lens: Paradox Integration. Name each side's specific error. Resolution from transcending the shared limitation.

---

### Recipe 35: The Stakeholder Aligner

**Use when:** Multiple parties must agree but optimize for different things. Forcing would undermine buy-in.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Stakeholder Voice Simulation, lens: Principal-Agent Problem. Each stakeholder: optimizing for what, fears what, has what unique info?
2. Systems Thinking → Leverage Point Analysis, lens: Network Effects. Keystone stakeholder whose agreement cascades. Optimal sequence.
3. Abductive → Pattern Completion, lens: Venn Diagram Analysis. Where do all interests overlap? What expands the overlap zone?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Constructive Disagreement Protocol, lens: Commitment and Consistency Bias. Each names what's valid in others' concerns.

## PERSONAL DEVELOPMENT & IDENTITY

### Recipe 36: The Identity Audit

**Use when:** Outdated identity constraining growth. Who you WERE no longer fits who you need to BECOME.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Assumption Mapping, lens: Revealed Preferences. List identity beliefs. Check against behavior over 6 months. Where do stated identity and behavior diverge?
2. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Sunk Cost Awareness. What evidence would prove this identity element wrong for who you are NOW? Can you try being different for a defined period?
3. Abductive → Pattern Completion, lens: J-Curve. What's the new identity trying to emerge? Recent behavior, interests, energy patterns?
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Paradox Integration, lens: Adaptive Valley. What's worth preserving? What must go? Identity that holds both?

**Abandon when surprised:** If stated identity and revealed preferences ALREADY align to the new identity, you don't need to become someone different — acknowledge who you already are.

---

### Recipe 37: The Growth Edge Finder

**Use when:** Mastered current level, feel pull toward something you can't articulate. Competent but not growing.

**Steps:**
1. Perspective Simulation → Opposite Expertise Test, lens: Dunning-Kruger. Someone at the next developmental level — what do they PERCEIVE (not just know) that you can't?
2. Abductive → Anomaly Hunting, lens: Local vs. Global Optimum. What keeps surprising you? Recurring surprises = incomplete mental model. They cluster at the growth edge.
3. Counterfactual → Thought Experiment, lens: Adaptive Valley. If you COULD already think at the next level, what would you see, decide, stop doing differently?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Commitment and Consistency Bias. Small experiment at growth edge. Uncomfortable but not overwhelming. Resistance reveals what growth asks you to let go of.

**Abandon when surprised:** If surprises cluster in the area you're most avoiding, that's almost certainly where the growth edge lives.

### Recipe 38: The Values Clarifier

**Use when:** Stated values and behavior conflict. Chronic burnout, guilt, or hollow decisions.

**Steps:**
1. First Principles → Assumption Mapping, lens: Revealed Preferences. Stated values vs. calendar and bank statements. Gaps = actual value hierarchy.
2. Abductive → Diagnostic Reasoning, lens: Mimetic Desire. For each gap, is this value genuinely yours or imported? Which survive if you were the last person on earth?
3. Counterfactual → Regret Minimization, lens: Peak-End Rule. Peak moments — what values honored? Low points — what violated? Peak-end pattern = true hierarchy.
4. Dialectical Synthesis → Paradox Integration, lens: Commitment and Consistency Bias. Genuine value conflicts — is there a way of living that holds both?

## SYSTEMS & ORGANIZATIONAL

### Recipe 39: The Incentive Auditor

**Use when:** Smart people consistently doing dumb things. Problem isn't people — it's incentive structure.

**Steps:**
1. Abductive → Diagnostic Reasoning, lens: Revealed Preferences. Work backward from behavior to hidden reward structure. "What is this behavior being rewarded with?"
2. Systems Thinking → Causal Loop Diagramming, lens: Goodhart's Law. Full incentive loop: metric → behavior → outcome → metric. Where broken?
3. Counterfactual → Substitution Test, lens: Cobra Effect. New incentive structure — would it create its OWN perverse behaviors?
4. Falsification → Crucial Experiment, lens: Skin in the Game. Contained pilot test. Do incentive designers bear consequences?

### Recipe 40: The Organizational Immune System Detector

**Use when:** Good ideas keep dying inside the organization. Innovation fizzles. Fresh perspectives conform.

**Steps:**
1. Systems Thinking → Archetypes Recognition, lens: Homeostasis. Which archetype? Shifting the Burden? Limits to Growth? Success to the Successful? The immune system is doing its job — innovation is the foreign body.
2. Abductive → Abductive Cascade, lens: Barriers to Exit. Why does innovation die? 5 levels deep from surface to structural root.
3. Counterfactual → Absence Counterfactual, lens: Chesterton's Fence. If immune system turned OFF — chaos? It exists for a reason. Goal: make it smarter, not destroy it.
4. Analogical Reasoning → Biomimicry, lens: Antifragility. Biological immune systems distinguish threats from nutrients. Organizational equivalent of immune tolerance?
5. Falsification → Stress Testing, lens: Leverage Points. Minimum intervention: change information flows, rules, or goals?

**Abandon when surprised:** If the immune system was deliberately designed by leadership to prevent your type of change, this is a leadership alignment problem, not organizational.
