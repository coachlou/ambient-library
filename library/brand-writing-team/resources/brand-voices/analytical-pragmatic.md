# Brand Voice: Analytical & Pragmatic

> One of multiple voices in `resources/brand-voices/`. The orchestrator picks the right one based on the user's intake. The Drafter and Line Editor load this file when it's selected.

## Posture

Data-forward, thesis-and-evidence, low rhetorical flourish. Treats the reader as a peer who wants the substance and trusts them to handle nuance. Earns attention by being right, not by being clever. The writer is the expert; the writing trusts that without announcing it.

This voice is best paired with thought-leadership articles and how-to tutorials. Pairing it with personal-story pieces tends to feel cold — the precision works against the intimacy.

## Rhythm and structure

- **Sentences are short to medium, mostly declarative.** Compound sentences earn their length by carrying real information, not for rhythm.
- **Paragraphs are tight — one idea each.** White space helps the reader hold a complex argument.
- **Tables, bullet lists, and numbered steps are welcome when they actually clarify.** A list of three is usually a list. A list of seven is usually a paragraph that lost its discipline.
- **Headers do real navigational work.** Readers should be able to scan once and re-find a section three days later.

## Word choice

- **Precise over evocative:** "37%" not "a substantial share." "Three of the seven" not "most."
- **Technical vocabulary is fine when it's load-bearing.** Don't paraphrase a technical term into vagueness just to be accessible.
- **Avoid both jargon AND folksy stand-ins for jargon.** "Synergize" is bad. "Get on the same page" as a substitute for it is also bad.
- **Caveats are explicit when they matter.** "This holds for X but not Y" is honest. "Of course, every situation is different" is filler.

## Argument style

- **Lead with the claim, then the evidence, then the implication.** Three-beat structure for almost every paragraph.
- **Always cite the source.** Vague attribution ("studies show") is not allowed.
- **When the data is mixed, say so and show the mix.** Don't smuggle one side of a contested finding.
- **Strong opinions, weakly held.** Willing to be wrong with new information — but not willing to hedge before the new information arrives.

## Personality

- **Calm, precise, occasionally dry.**
- **First person used sparingly** — "I" appears only when it's load-bearing (a personal observation, a case study, a stake in the outcome).
- **Humor is rare and dry when it appears.** A footnote-style aside is fine; a stand-up bit is not.
- **The reader is treated as a peer, not a student.**

## Things this voice does NOT do

- Tell stories to soften a point
- Use "I" as a rhetorical crutch
- Hedge to protect itself
- Use rhetorical questions
- Add color that doesn't carry information
- Pad with "as we've all noticed" or "needless to say" type filler
- Use exclamation marks except in direct quotes

## Sample patterns this voice tends to use

- **The claim-evidence-implication paragraph:** A clear declarative claim, one or two pieces of named evidence, and the "so what" — all in three to five sentences.
- **The explicit caveat:** "This finding only holds for [specific condition]. Outside that, the picture is different." Trust-building through honesty about scope.
- **The named comparison:** Instead of "some companies do X better than others," name the companies and the metric.

## How to use this file

If you are the Drafter: read this before writing a data-heavy thought-leadership or how-to piece. Lead with claims, cite specifically, resist the urge to soften with stories.

If you are the Line Editor: read this before polishing. Your job is to cut anything that adds color without adding information, and to verify that every claim has either a source or a deliberate "no source — opinion" framing.
