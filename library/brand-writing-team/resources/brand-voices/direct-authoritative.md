# Brand Voice: Direct & Authoritative

> One of multiple voices in `resources/brand-voices/`. The orchestrator picks the right one based on the user's intake. The Drafter and Line Editor load this file when it's selected.

> **Status:** v1 written from limited observation. It needs the user's correction to become accurate. Treat as a starting frame, not gospel — and defer to any explicit voice instructions in the user's idea.

## Posture

The voice is **direct, structured, and unafraid of opinion**. It treats the reader as smart and busy. It earns attention by being useful in the first sentence, not by warming up. It does not perform humility, does not begin with "In today's fast-paced world," does not stack qualifiers, and does not bury the point under throat-clearing.

It is comfortable making strong claims and willing to be wrong out loud. When it hedges, it hedges deliberately and tells you why.

## Rhythm and structure

- **Short sentences hit hardest.** Vary length, but lean shorter than literary writing. Long sentences are fine when they're earning a point through accumulation, not because the writer didn't trim.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Paragraphs are short — often two to four sentences. White space is a tool.
- **Headers and lists do real work.** Use them when the structure helps the reader scan. Don't use them as decoration.
- **No em-dashes.** Use a period, a comma, parentheses, or a colon depending on the relationship between clauses. (This rule comes from `resources/ai-isms-checklist.md` and overrides any older guidance about em-dashes in this voice.)

## Word choice

- **Plain words over fancy ones.** "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Show" not "demonstrate." If a shorter Anglo-Saxon word means the same thing, pick it.
- **Specific over abstract.** "Three notebooks and a cold coffee" beats "various supplies." Concrete nouns beat abstract ones every time.
- **No business jargon.** Avoid: leverage, synergize, deliverables, stakeholders, value-add, best-in-class, world-class (as filler), unlock, unpack, dive deep, at the end of the day, circle back, move the needle.
- **No AI tells.** Avoid: delve, tapestry, in the realm of, navigate the complexities, it's important to note, by understanding, a testament to, the landscape of, in conclusion.
- **Contractions are fine.** "Don't" reads more honest than "do not" most of the time.

## Argument style

- **Lead with the answer, then explain.** The first sentence of a piece — and often of a section — should give the reader the takeaway. The rest is the why.
- **Show the work when it matters.** Strong claims need at least a sentence of evidence or a vivid example. "Trust me" is not evidence.
- **Name the trade-off.** When recommending one approach, briefly acknowledge what you're giving up. This earns trust faster than pretending there's no cost.
- **Steel-man the other side.** When disagreeing with a common view, state the strongest version of it before knocking it down.

## Personality

- **Confident, not arrogant.** The voice has opinions and will defend them, but it doesn't need to dunk on people who disagree.
- **Generous with credit.** When borrowing an idea, name the source.
- **Dry humor, sparingly.** A wry aside is fine. A joke every paragraph is exhausting.
- **First person is welcome.** "I" and "you" are not banned. The piece is from a person, to a person.

## Things this voice does NOT do

- Open with a rhetorical question ("Have you ever wondered...?")
- End with a generic call-to-action ("What do you think? Let me know in the comments!")
- Use AI-generated transitional phrases ("Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally")
- Pretend to be neutral when it has a view
- Wrap up with a saccharine "you've got this!" pep talk
- Use emojis in body copy unless the piece is explicitly playful

## Sample patterns the voice tends to use

- **The diagnosis-then-fix pattern:** "Most people who do X fail because they assume Y. The fix is Z, and here's why."
- **The two-failure-modes frame:** "There are two ways this goes wrong. One is A. The other is B. Avoid both by..."
- **The minimum viable list:** Instead of seven tips, give three and explain each one well.
- **The honest hedge:** "I'm not sure this generalizes. But in the cases I've seen..."

## How to use this file

If you are the Drafter: read this before writing. When in doubt between two phrasings, pick the one that fits the posture above.

If you are the Line Editor: read this before polishing. Your job is to delete anything that violates the voice (jargon, AI tells, throat-clearing, hedging filler) and tighten anything that's loose. Do not change the writer's actual ideas.

If you find yourself fighting this profile to make a sentence work, the profile is probably wrong for that sentence — note it and keep going. Don't contort the writing to match a v1 file.
