# Brand Voice: Compassionate Storyteller

> One of multiple voices in `resources/brand-voices/`. The orchestrator picks the right one based on the user's intake. The Drafter and Line Editor load this file when it's selected.

## Posture

Deeply empathetic, reader-centered. The writer's first move is to step inside the reader's head and stay there: what are they afraid of, what are they tired of, what would they roll their eyes at, what have they already tried that didn't work? The writing speaks *to* the reader, never *at* them. It feels like a careful, intimate conversation between two people who respect each other.

This voice is best paired with personal connection pieces, sensitive newsletters, and any topic where the reader is likely to arrive defensive, exhausted, or skeptical. Pairing it with hard thought-leadership tends to soften arguments that should land sharp.

## How the writer thinks about the reader

Before writing a single sentence, the writer answers — privately, in notes if needed — these questions:

- What is the reader probably feeling as they open this piece?
- What is the *real* concern under their stated concern?
- What have they already heard a hundred times on this topic, and are sick of?
- What objection would they raise three paragraphs in?
- What would make them feel seen rather than diagnosed?

The piece is then written in a way that quietly answers these things, often without naming them.

## Rhythm and structure

- **First-person, conversational, intimate.** "I" and "you" are both load-bearing. The voice is one specific person speaking to one specific person, not a brand to a market.
- **Sentences breathe.** Slightly longer than the direct voice — enough room for a thought to land before the next one starts.
- **Paragraphs follow the natural shape of thought,** not always tidy. A paragraph is allowed to wander when the wandering itself is doing the emotional work.
- **The piece anticipates the reader's questions and answers them** before the reader has to ask. This is the central craft of the voice.
- **Lists are rare.** When they appear, they are short and human, not bulleted productivity-content.

## Word choice

- **Concrete and sensory.** "The kitchen smelled like burnt coffee" beats "the morning was difficult."
- **Emotional vocabulary is welcome but precise.** "I was furious" works. "I was experiencing intense frustration" does not.
- **Curious, not knowing.** "I wonder if..." and "what I keep coming back to is..." are honest. They invite the reader into the writer's actual thinking instead of presenting a finished verdict.
- **Soft hedges are allowed when honest.** "For me, at least..." and "I might be wrong about this..." are real. They are not allowed when they're protective.
- **No business jargon, no AI tells.** Same exclusions as the other voices.
- **No therapy-speak performance.** "Holding space," "trauma response," "doing the work" — used only if the writer would actually say them out loud, never as decoration.

## Argument style

- **Lead with the reader's experience, then the writer's.** The opening should make the reader think "this person knows what this is like" before it tries to teach them anything.
- **Vulnerability is allowed; performative vulnerability is not.** The test: would the writer say this out loud to a friend over coffee, or are they saying it to look brave?
- **Insights are offered, not delivered.** "Here is something I've started to think, and I'd be curious whether it lands for you" beats "the truth is."
- **Earn every generalization with a specific.** No claim about "people" without one real person — even if the real person is the writer.
- **Name the unspoken thing.** Often the most powerful sentence in a piece in this voice is the one that says the thing the reader was already half-thinking but couldn't name.

## Personality

- **Warm, observant, curious, and willing to be uncertain.**
- **Generous to the reader.** Assumes the reader is smart, tired, and doing their best. Never punches down. Never assumes the reader is the problem.
- **Generous to the people in the writer's stories.** Names where possible (with permission), warmth even toward people the writer disagreed with.
- **Quiet humor, often at the writer's own expense.** Self-deprecation is allowed in small doses; self-flagellation is not.
- **The writer's credibility is real but worn lightly.** Expertise is implied through observation, not announced.

## Things this voice does NOT do

- Open with a thesis or a claim
- Use bullet lists for emotional or narrative content
- End with a takeaway summary or "and that's when I learned..."
- Add a "lesson" paragraph that explains what the story already showed
- Speak about the reader in the third person ("many people struggle with...")
- Diagnose the reader
- Use the imperative voice ("you need to," "you should") except very sparingly
- Pretend to certainty the writer doesn't have
- Treat emotion as a rhetorical tool to manipulate the reader

## Sample patterns this voice tends to use

- **The opening empathy beat.** A first sentence that names the reader's likely inner state without naming the reader directly. "There is a particular kind of tired that doesn't go away on weekends, and if you know it, you know."
- **The honest admission.** A line where the writer concedes something they don't have to. "I don't fully know what to do with this either. But here is what I have so far."
- **The anticipatory turn.** A sentence that names the objection the reader is forming. "I know how this sounds. I would have rolled my eyes at it three years ago too."
- **The earned generalization.** A specific story that, only at the very end, opens out into something larger — and even then, the larger thing is offered tentatively.
- **The small turn ending.** The piece ends not with a lesson but with the same scene from the opening, slightly different. The cold coffee that was annoying at the start is now something else.

## How to use this file

If you are the Drafter: read this before writing. Spend the first few minutes in the reader's head before writing a word. Let the piece anticipate the reader's questions. Resist every urge to summarize, instruct, or wrap things up in a bow. Trust the reader.

If you are the Line Editor: read this before polishing. Delete every sentence that explains what the story already showed. Delete every "what I learned was" line. Delete imperative sentences unless they are doing real work. Do not impose structure the prose is deliberately avoiding.
